# Sculpture Work

These pieces were created in a sculpture class where I focused on how form, material, and placement affect the overall experience of an object.

While working on these, I paid attention to things like balance, proportion, texture, and how different materials behave physically.
I also started thinking more about how small decisions—such as positioning, contrast, and repetition—can guide how someone looks at and interprets a piece.

Through this process, I became more interested in how structure and material can communicate ideas without relying on text.

---

##  Cardboard Tree

<p align="center">
  <img src="images/tree-cardboard.png" width="450"/>
</p>

I used corrugated cardboard to build this tree, keeping the white inner layer visible.
Since paper comes from trees, I liked the idea of turning it back into a tree form again.

The white surface reminded me of a tree covered in snow, so I kept the material raw instead of painting it.
I focused on layering to build texture and make the trunk and branches feel more natural.

##  Hand & Foot

<p align="center">

<p align="center">
  <img src="images/hand-foot-sculpture-3.png" width="400"/>
</p>

This piece is based on a small memory from when I was younger.  
I couldn’t bend my toes, and I thought something was wrong with my foot, while my sister would make fun of it.

I used casts of my actual hand and foot, and intentionally hung the foot so that the toes become the main focus.  
The hand is pointing directly at it, almost like saying “look at this.”

It has a slightly playful but also ironic tone, turning a small insecurity into something visible and almost exaggerated.

The clean white surface helps draw more attention to the form, while the dark base keeps the focus on the objects.

##  Abstract Connection

<p align="center">
  <img src="images/abstract-connection-sculpture.png" width="450"/>
</p>

This piece started from thinking about how people are connected.

The figures are intentionally simple — some feel more “empty,” while others feel more solid — but they are all holding hands. I wanted to show that even different kinds of people can still be connected.

The forms were carved and shaped from plywood, and I focused on creating soft curves instead of sharp edges to keep the overall structure more continuous and organic.

I added small hanging elements above to give a lighter and slightly playful feeling.  
It’s not meant to have one fixed meaning, but more of a sense of support and togetherness.
